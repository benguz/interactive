What rights does a person have to their voice?

In the 1988 case Midler vs Ford Motor co., the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit decided that a voice is inherently uncopyrightable and intellectual property protections do not extend to ownership of a voice since the voice is not fixed in a tangible medium of expression as required by the statute. In this case, singer Bette Midler had brought Ford Motor co. to court over using an impersonator of her distinct, recognizable, and marketable voice in a highly successful commercial without compensation. Though the court acknowledged the distinctness and fundamental role voice plays in the identity of an individual, it concluded that "mere imitation of a recorded performance would not constitute a copyright infringement even where one performer deliberately sets out to simulate another's performance as exactly as possible." In consequence, individuals who have been voice copied or cloned have not been able to use copyright to ensure protection or recompense no matter the mechanism of replication. [Wells-Edwards]

Can more general identity protections prevent voice cloning?

The right of publicity provides intellectual property protection against the misappropriation of a person’s name, likeness, or other component personal identity for commercial benefit. Though the laws detailing the right of publicity varies state by state, these protections will still generally only be applicable to well-known celebrities and public figures in limited cases. Firstly, there exists the implication in the law that the subject of the voice cloning has an identity worth appropriating. Therefore lesser-known vocalists are left vulnerable. Secondly, the right of publicity protections only extends as far as is necessary to encourage entertainment, much in the same manner as patents. It is there to allow the performer to reap the rewards of their efforts with little care for the feelings or reputation of the performer. Therefore while this might provide an avenue for recompense if proof that the cloner has benefited from the appropriation is presented, the ethical and legal concerns of the creation of the clone in the first place is left unaddressed. Certainly, vocalistis should be compensated if their unique voices are utilized to train and produce goods provided by AI voice cloning technology companies.

How can advancements in voice cloning inform new laws?

Alteration of copyright laws is necessary in order for vocal artists, known and unknown alike, to receive protection for their livelihoods and reap the benefits of the styles and skills that have curated throughout their endeavors. At the same time, vocal cloning must be recognized as an important and emerging tool for creativity and free expression. Moving forward, the state must carefully consider its role as a regulator and the various social interpretations of the technology as it re-examines and develops laws.

Tennessee has been an early leader in this regard with the development of the Ensuring Likeness Voice and Image Security (ELVIS) Act. This bill updates Tennessee’s Protection of Personal Rights law to specifically include impersonations of voice using AI. This reality reflects how AI has disrupted the stabilized interpretation of tools of voice mimicry in the music industry, disruption which will occur throughout the United States and the world.

A new period of interpretation is developing and we must wait to see how it stabilizes and closes. Key to this idea, though, is that the state can help shape the stabilization of this new interpretation to ensure the best interests of the various stakeholders.
