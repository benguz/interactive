Artists  

AI has been increasingly used in music production, distribution, and creation since it was first tested out in the 1950s [Brown]. However, recent developments in AI technology and copyright law have renewed questions about the appropriate use of AI in music. Voice-cloning has been a particularly contentious issue as artists large and small confront technology companies over their rights to protection and compensation. By walking you through the concerns shared by music artists, the current theoretical and applied states of copyright laws on voice, and providing the opportunity to experience voice cloning for yourself, this interactive project aims to convey the need for updated legal protections on an artist’s voice and an ethical standard for the training data used by AI tech companies.


The plight of the vocalist as AI voice cloning grows is two-fold. For one, artists feel a more personal threat to their autonomy, dignity, and reputation [Antifos, Fekadu]. On the other side, artists grow concerned over the economic threat of AI [Chow, Hammond, Berry]. Explore the information below for an expanded explanation of how these threats come into being.

Personal Threat

Appropriating a deeply personal quality

The voice is a profoundly personal aspect of one’s identity [Wells-Edwards]. For the majority of the population it is the core medium of self expression, therefore to have one’s voice appropriated or ‘stolen’ is a violation of one’s essential boundaries of autonomy [Nathanson].

No rest for the weary

Even after passing, artists can be “resurrected” to perform once again. This can be disturbing for individuals hold the memory of artists as sacred and prefer to let the dead rest.

Economic Threat

Rapidly produced competition

Vocal tracks can be created at a much more rapid pace which can, in many cases, compete with the original work of the artist. [Chow]

Use without compensation

Voice clones of well-known celebrities have been created by training models on audio content of the original person without her permission [Chow].
